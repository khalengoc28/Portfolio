Background & Research Interests

I am a Vietnamese-born mathematician and aspiring neuroscientist studying at Wabash College, where I am completing a B.A. in Mathematics with a concentration in Neuroscience (expected May 2026). My academic trajectory has been shaped by a deep fascination with how the brain transforms sensory evidence into decisions and motor actions — a question that lives at the crossroads of applied mathematics, cognitive science, and computational neuroscience.

At Wabash, I work with Dr. Neil Schmitzer-Torbert on the behavioral and oculomotor correlates of deliberation. Our project uses eye-tracking (Tobii 5th-Gen) and a translational virtual navigation foraging task to characterize vicarious trial-and-error (VTE) — the head-scanning behavior observed in rodents at choice points — in human participants (n = 56). By analyzing gaze patterns, dwelling times, and movement trajectories, we investigate how visual sampling signatures map onto the deliberative processes previously documented in animal models. This line of work bridges comparative psychology and decision neuroscience, asking whether the same computational principles governing rodent navigation extend to human choice behavior.

During the summer of 2025, I joined Dr. Joshua Cashaback's Motor Control & Decision-Making Lab at the University of Delaware as a research intern. There I trained intensively in the neuromechanics of human motion — optimal feedback control, linear-quadratic Gaussian regulators, Kalman filtering, and cost-function optimization — and replicated computational models of urgency-gated decisions within the token-task framework. My ongoing project designs mathematical models to explain how humans optimally plan and execute reaching movements when facing multiple potential targets simultaneously, linking motor planning with the dynamics of evidence accumulation.

Together, these two research threads form a coherent program: Dr. Schmitzer-Torbert's work lets me probe where people look and how long they deliberate before committing to a choice, while Dr. Cashaback's work lets me model how that deliberation unfolds in the motor system as the hand moves toward a target. The common thread is understanding decision-making as a dynamic, embodied process — one that can be captured by rigorous mathematical frameworks.

My broader research interests include computational and theoretical neuroscience, stochastic processes in neural decision circuits, sensorimotor integration, and the application of mathematical modeling to questions in cognitive and behavioral science. I am actively preparing to pursue graduate study (M.S./Ph.D.) in computational neuroscience or a related quantitative discipline.
