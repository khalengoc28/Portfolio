Vicarious Trial-and-Error Through Gaze Behavior & Deliberation in a Translational Virtual Navigation Task

Kha Le, Elijah Arnold & N. Schmitzer-Torbert

Code repository coming soon. Check back for R analysis scripts, eye-tracking data processing pipelines, and computational models.

Vicarious trial-and-error (VTE) is a behavioral phenomenon first observed in rodents at choice points during navigation, characterized by deliberative head-scanning movements. This project investigates whether analogous gaze-based VTE signatures emerge in human participants performing a translational virtual navigation foraging task.

We recruited 56 participants and used Tobii 5th-generation eye-tracking to capture gaze behavior during an online virtual navigation paradigm. By analyzing gaze sampling patterns, fixation sequences, dwelling times, and movement trajectories, we characterize how visual information gathering maps onto deliberative decision processes in humans — bridging rodent and human models of deliberation.

University of Delaware · Summer 2025

Optimal Feedback Control & Urgency-Gated Decision Models in the Token Task Framework

Kha Le & J. Cashaback's Lab

Python notebooks and model replications coming soon — including OFC, LQG, and urgency-gated decision simulations.

During a 2.5-month summer internship at the University of Delaware's Health Science Complex, I worked under Dr. Joshua Cashaback in a lab spanning mechanical engineering, physics, and biomechanics. The project centered on building computational models of optimal feedback control (OFC) to investigate how lateral hand movements reflect deliberation prior to decision commitment.

Training covered the neuromechanics of human motion: optimal feedback control theory, redundant motor systems, dynamics, linear-quadratic Gaussian (LQG) regulators, Kalman filtering, and cost-function optimization in decision-making. I completed structured assignments in Python (VSCode & Google Colab) and then progressed to replicating four mathematical models of decision-making within the token task framework — a paradigm in which participants observe sequentially revealed tokens and decide when to commit to a left or right reach.

My task was to design mathematical models explaining how humans optimally plan and execute movements when facing multiple potential targets simultaneously. I also assisted with experimental data collection using the KINARM exoskeleton robot for musculoskeletal and virtual token tasks (n = 4) and participated in bi-weekly lab meetings critiquing project progress.
